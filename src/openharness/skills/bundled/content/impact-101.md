# impact-101

A beginner-friendly introduction to impact investing and the key frameworks used by Impact Vision.

## When to use

Use when the user is new to impact investing, unfamiliar with IRIS+/SDG terminology,
or asks "what is..." or "explain..." about impact measurement concepts.

## What is Impact Investing?

Impact investing means investing with the intention to generate **positive, measurable
social and environmental impact** alongside a financial return. It sits between
traditional philanthropy (grants with no financial return) and conventional investing
(financial return only).

Key principles:
- **Intentionality**: The investor specifically intends to create positive impact
- **Measurement**: Impact must be measurable, not just claimed
- **Additionality**: The investment should cause impact that wouldn't happen otherwise
- **Financial return**: Unlike grants, the investor expects capital back (+ returns)

## Key Frameworks Explained

### IRIS+ (Impact Reporting and Investment Standards)

**What**: A catalog of ~787 standardized metrics for measuring social/environmental impact.
Maintained by the GIIN (Global Impact Investing Network).

**Why it matters**: Without standardized metrics, every company reports impact differently,
making comparison impossible. IRIS+ is the "GAAP for impact."

**Example metrics**:
- OI1479: Number of smallholder farmers who received agricultural support
- PI4060: Number of individuals with access to financial services for the first time
- OI8869: Greenhouse gas emissions reduced/avoided (tons CO2e)

### UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

**What**: 17 global goals adopted by 193 countries in 2015, with 169 specific targets.
They cover everything from poverty (SDG 1) to climate (SDG 13) to partnerships (SDG 17).

**The 17 Goals**:
1. No Poverty
2. Zero Hunger
3. Good Health and Well-Being
4. Quality Education
5. Gender Equality
6. Clean Water and Sanitation
7. Affordable and Clean Energy
8. Decent Work and Economic Growth
9. Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure
10. Reduced Inequalities
11. Sustainable Cities and Communities
12. Responsible Consumption and Production
13. Climate Action
14. Life Below Water
15. Life on Earth
16. Peace, Justice and Strong Institutions
17. Partnerships for the Goals

**Why it matters**: SDGs provide a common language between investors, companies,
governments, and NGOs. When a fund says "we target SDG 7," everyone knows that
means clean energy.

### 5 Dimensions of Impact

Developed by the Impact Management Project (IMP), now the standard way to assess
impact quality:

| Dimension | Question | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **What** | What outcome occurs? | Increased income for farmers |
| **Who** | Who experiences it? | 5,000 smallholder farmers in rural Kenya |
| **How Much** | Scale, depth, duration? | 30% average income increase, sustained 3+ years |
| **Contribution** | Would it happen anyway? | No similar service exists in the region |
| **Risk** | What could go wrong? | Climate events could destroy crops |

### NESTA Standards of Evidence

How strong is the evidence behind an impact claim? 5 levels:

| Level | Description | Example |
|------:|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Narrative only | "We help farmers" (no data) |
| 2 | Output data | "We trained 500 farmers" (counts activities) |
| 3 | Outcome data | "Farmer income rose 30% (pre/post survey)" |
| 4 | Controlled comparison | "Treatment group income rose 30% vs 5% in control" |
| 5 | Rigorous evaluation | "Independent RCT confirmed 28% income increase (p<0.01)" |

Most startups are at Level 1-2. Level 3+ is considered credible impact evidence.

## ESG Frameworks

ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) frameworks overlap with impact investing but
focus more on risk management and disclosure:

| Framework | What it covers | Who uses it |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **SASB** | Industry-specific material topics (17 industries) | Public companies, asset managers |
| **GRI** | Comprehensive sustainability reporting (34 standards) | Companies of all sizes globally |
| **TCFD** | Climate risk disclosure (4 pillars) | Financial institutions, listed companies |
| **SFDR PAI** | EU mandatory sustainability indicators (14) | EU fund managers, asset owners |
| **EDCI** | PE/VC-specific ESG metrics (17) | Private equity, venture capital |
| **UNPRI** | Responsible investment principles (6) | Institutional investors, asset owners |

## Common Questions from Newcomers

**"What's the difference between ESG and impact?"**
ESG focuses on managing risks (does the company handle environmental/social/governance
risks well?). Impact focuses on creating positive outcomes (does the company make the
world better?). A company can have great ESG scores but low impact, or vice versa.

**"How do you measure something as vague as 'social impact'?"**
You break it down into specific, countable outcomes. Instead of "we help communities,"
you measure "number of households with improved access to clean water" (IRIS+ metric
PI2822) and compare against a baseline.

**"What's a Theory of Change?"**
A logical model showing: If we do [activities], then [outputs] happen, which lead to
[outcomes], which contribute to [long-term impact]. It's the causal story connecting
your work to the change you want to see.

**"What's impact washing?"**
Making impact claims without evidence. Red flags include: vague claims ("empowering
communities"), no baseline data, no measurement plan, breadth without depth (lots of
people touched but no evidence of change), and ignoring negative side effects.
